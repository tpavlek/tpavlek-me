Why should you vote for me for Edmonton City Council?

Stay tuned and subscribe for email updates so you'll know when a full platform is available. In the meantime, here's a few reasons why I deserve your vote in October of 2017.

I represent a truly different choice

Other candidates are usually picked from businessmen, lawyers or career politicians. If we keep electing the same types of people, with the same background, how can we be surprised when City Hall doesn't change?

As a candidate, I'm young enough that I'm not interested in a government salary while I run a business on the side and plan for retirement. I'm running to shape my city into somewhere I can live for the next seventy years. Short-term politically motivated decisions won't help me get there.

Professionally, I'm a software developer. My entire job is to write computer code to take care of the junk so smart people like analysts and tradesmen have more time to do the complex stuff that only they can. Our city is wrought with technological waste, but few of the members of Council even have the understanding to identify the problem, much less fix it. The Metro Line? The train that has been delaying passengers and commuters in the area for several years after it was supposed to be done? It's being held up by a software problem. The warning signs for this issue were everywhere, but without a technologically adept council, the red flags went unnoticed.

I'm committed to complete openness, not just transparency

Every candidate harps about "transparency", "openness" and "accountability" to score political points, but few of them have concrete actions to back it up. As a developer, I built YEGVotes.info, a website that lets you see your councillor's attendance at meetings, and every vote they have ever made. When I tell you I'm voting for something, I want you to have the tools to check. Can you just skip work whenever you want? Absolutely not. I've made attendance records at council very easy to access, because informed voters should know when their councillor doesn't show up for work.

If I make a claim like "we reduced service on a bus route because of low ridership" you shouldn't have to trust me. Edmonton has an award-winning open data catalogue that allows citizens and journalists to see data the City of Edmonton makes available. However, currently data sets must be added to the catalogue individually, and it is a long process to do so. Edmonton needs to embrace an "open-first" policy for our data. If the city has data, personally-identifiable information should be automatically removed, and the data should be made available to the public immediately.

I want zero serious collisions on our roadways

The City of Edmonton claims to employ a strategy called "Vision Zero" - but they've been halfhearted at best. The entire program feels like a bait-and-switch. I believe in true Vision Zero, which means that no loss of life in our roadways is acceptable. True Vision Zero believes that humans will make errors, and roadway design should minimize those errors.

What does that mean in terms of policy? Well, on inner-city streets, we have to slow down. I will advocate for 40km/h speed limits in residential neighbourhoods as I am already doing in my own neighbourhood of Hazeldean. But that means that roadway design must change as well. Large, flat, straight roadways should not exist where cars are mingling with pedestrians, as the natural feeling on those roadways is to go fast. Every mode of transportation needs their own space, including pedestrians, cyclists and cars and roadway design needs to make interaction points safe and convenient. Tools like raised intersections, extended curbs, well-marked crosswalks and traffic calming in congested areas can all be applied to make the road safer for all users, without sending a photo radar bill in the mail to 500,000 people a year.

In the same vein, our large freeways that were designed for high speeds without pedestrian interaction need to be looked at. If traffic can safely move faster on those roadways it should. Photo Radar should be a last resort to make sure our people are safe. That means it should be deployed in areas with low-speeds and high pedestrian traffic. Penalising commuters on the Henday for moving safely with the flow of traffic is not what vision zero is about.

I'm ready to dedicate and communicate

I don't have kids. I'm sure I will one day, but this decade is for myself and my own life, not raising another. As many of you with families know, when you're raising kids you want to spend an incredible amount of time with them. Their needs become priority number one. I can serve Edmonton as priority number one - and whether I'm elected or not, making Edmonton better is something I'll continue to do.

I have the tools to communicate better. I'm adept at web-development and blogging for people who like to read their news on a website. I host a local municipal politics podcast, so I can keep constituents updated on their drive to work. For those on Twitter, I tweet regularly and engage in discussions there. My email inbox gets prompt replies and, of course, I have a mouth and a face so good ol' regular conversations are in my wheel house. I know there's not just one way to communicate anymore, and I'm ready to engage with my constituents however they would like to talk to me.

And so much more...
